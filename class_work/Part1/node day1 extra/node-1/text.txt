The Solar System is a vast and intricate system of celestial bodies centered around the Sun, a G-type main-sequence star that contains more than 99.8% of the total mass of the entire system. It is located in a small arm of the Milky Way galaxy called the Orion Arm, approximately 27,000 light-years from the galactic center.

The primary constituents of the Solar System are the eight major planets, which are divided into two distinct categories: the inner terrestrial planets and the outer gas giants. The terrestrial planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars—are composed mostly of rock and metal and have relatively high densities, slow rotation, solid surfaces, and few or no moons. In contrast, the gas giants—Jupiter and Saturn—and the ice giants—Uranus and Neptune—are much larger, primarily composed of hydrogen, helium, water, ammonia, and methane, and possess numerous moons, along with ring systems composed of rock, ice, and dust particles.

Additionally, the Solar System includes dwarf planets such as Pluto, which lies in the Kuiper Belt—a region populated with frozen bodies beyond Neptune where thousands of other small, icy objects reside. Closer to the Sun, between Mars and Jupiter, lies the asteroid belt, a region filled with numerous metallic and rocky bodies.

The influence of the Solar System extends far beyond the planets and asteroids, out to the heliosphere, a vast bubble in space produced by the solar wind, a stream of charged particles emanating from the Sun. This heliosphere helps shield the planets from cosmic radiation, playing a crucial role in the stability and composition of the Solar System.
